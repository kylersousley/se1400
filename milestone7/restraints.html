Roller coaster restraints are safety systems designed to keep riders secure while allowing enough freedom of movement to experience the ride’s sensations comfortably. The most common types include lap bars, which rest over the rider’s lower body and are often used on coasters focused on airtime or smooth maneuvers, and over-the-shoulder restraints (OTSRs), which provide upper-body security for rides with intense inversions or rapid directional changes. Many modern coasters use more ergonomic designs like soft vest-style restraints or articulated lap bars that minimize discomfort and headbanging while maintaining safety. Restraints are typically locked and monitored electronically, ensuring they fasten correctly before dispatch and remain secure throughout the ride. Together, these systems balance comfort, freedom, and protection, making it possible for riders to enjoy the full range of coaster elements confidently and safely.

Rollercoaster Restraints

Ratchet Restraints

Ratchet restraints use a mechanical pawl-and-gear system to lock securely into place as the rider pulls the bar down. Inside the restraint mechanism is a notched metal gear (the ratchet) and a spring-loaded pawl that drops into each tooth as the bar moves downward, creating the familiar clicking sound. The pawl allows movement in only one direction—down—preventing the bar from lifting back up unless the system is intentionally released by the ride operator. These mechanisms are typically backed by hydraulic or electronic sensors that confirm the pawl is fully engaged before the train can dispatch. Because each click corresponds to a fixed locking position, ratchet restraints can accommodate varying body sizes while ensuring a strong, fail-safe hold throughout the ride.

Hydraulic Restraints

Hydraulic restraints use pressurized fluid systems to create a smooth, adjustable, and highly secure hold on riders. Instead of locking into fixed “clicks” like a ratchet restraint, a hydraulic cylinder allows the restraint bar to move down freely but resist upward motion through fluid pressure. When the bar is lowered, hydraulic valves direct fluid into chambers that create increasing resistance, holding the bar firmly at whatever position it stops. This allows for precise, custom fitment for each rider’s body shape without the incremental locking of a ratchet. The system is monitored by pressure sensors that verify the restraint is within a safe operating range before the ride can dispatch. Because of their smooth operation and consistent pressure, hydraulic restraints are often used on coasters and thrill rides where comfort, flexibility, and reliability are equally important.
